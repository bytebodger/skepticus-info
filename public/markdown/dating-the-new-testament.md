When tracking down the earliest **complete** manuscripts of the New Testament, it is critical to separate *fragments* from *complete books*. While we have 2nd-century papyrus fragments containing a few verses (like the famous, credit-card-sized $\^{52}$ for the Gospel of John), we don't see fully intact, unbroken copies of individual books until the late 3rd to mid-4th centuries.

The survival of these complete texts comes down to three major manuscript discoveries:

1. **Papyrus 72 ($\^{72}$):** A 3rd-century papyrus codex containing the earliest complete copies of 1–2 Peter and Jude.
2. **Codex Vaticanus (B):** A parchment Bible dated to c. 325–350 AD. It is the earliest nearly complete New Testament, but it was damaged over time and is missing its final pages.
3. **Codex Sinaiticus ($\aleph$):** Dated slightly later to c. 330–360 AD. It is the oldest surviving manuscript to contain the *entire* New Testament in a single volume, making it the default earliest copy for any books missing from Vaticanus.
