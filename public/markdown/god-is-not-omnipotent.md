The concept of absolute omnipotence—the philosophical idea that God can do absolutely anything without limitation—is a later theological development. When you look at the raw text of the Bible, particularly older layers of the Hebrew Bible and specific New Testament passages, you find an entity whose power is frequently depicted as localized, contingent, or structurally limited.

Here's a detailed analysis of the primary passages that directly undermine the concept of an omnipotent deity.

---

## 1. Technological and Geographical Limitations

### Judges 1:19

> *"The LORD was with the men of Judah. They took possession of the hill country, but they were unable to drive the people from the plains, because they had chariots fitted with iron."*

* **The Analysis:** Apologists try to clean this up by claiming the pronoun "they" refers strictly to the human army of Judah, meaning human faith failed, not God's power. But the text explicitly links the presence and backing of Yahweh (*"The LORD was with the men of Judah"*) to the military campaign. Despite divine backing, the operation stalled because the enemy had superior military technology (iron chariots).
* **The Subtext:** This reflects early Ancient Near Eastern henotheism. Yahweh wasn't originally viewed as an omnipresent, omnipotent cosmic ruler; he was a localized war deity of the mountains and hills. This is explicitly confirmed later in **1 Kings 20:23**, where the Arameans state, *"Their gods are gods of the hills. That is why they were too strong for us. But if we fight them on the plains, surely we will be stronger than they."* The narrative in Judges 1:19 shows that iron chariots on flat ground successfully checked divine military power.

---

## 2. Physical and Combat Vulnerability

### Genesis 32:25–28

> *"When the man saw that he could not overpower him, he touched the socket of Jacob’s hip so that his hip was wrenched as he wrestled with the man... Then the man said, 'Your name will no longer be Jacob, but Israel, because you have struggled with God and with humans and have overcome.'"*

* **The Analysis:** Jacob engages in a literal, physical wrestling match with a figure later identified as God (*"I saw God face to face,"* v. 30). The text explicitly notes that the divine manifestation realized he *could not* overpower a mortal man through sheer strength. To end the stalemate, the entity has to resort to a tactical physical exploit (dislocating Jacob's hip joint).
* **The Subtext:** If an entity's physical strength has a ceiling that can be matched by a determined human male to the point of a stalemate, that entity isn't omnipotent. It possesses localized, corporeal limitations.

---

## 3. Power Contingent on Human Belief

### Mark 6:5

> *"He could not do any miracles there, except lay his hands on a few sick people and heal them."*

* **The Analysis:** In orthodox Christian theology, Jesus is fully God, possessing the divine nature. Yet, during his visit to Nazareth, the text doesn't say Jesus *chose* not to do miracles; it says he *could not* (*ouk edynato*—was not able, lacked the power). The text explicitly anchors this systemic failure to the people's lack of faith (v. 6).
* **The Subtext:** True omnipotence operates unilaterally. If divine power can be throttled, blocked, or rendered entirely ineffective by a human psychological state (skepticism), then that power isn't absolute. It's dependent on an external variable.

---

## 4. Ontological and Structural Inabilities

### Titus 1:2 & Hebrews 6:18

> *"...God, who cannot lie..."* / *"...it is impossible for God to lie..."*

### James 1:13

> *"For God cannot be tempted by evil, nor does he tempt anyone."*

* **The Analysis:** Classical theologians argue these aren't "weaknesses" but "perfections"—meaning God's nature is so perfectly good that he is incapable of doing things contrary to that nature. However, from a strict definition of omnipotence (the ability to execute any logically possible action), this is a structural constraint.
* **The Subtext:** Lying is a logically possible communicative act; humans do it effortlessly. Temptation is a logically possible psychological state. If God structurally lacks the agency or capacity to execute a lie or experience temptation, his portfolio of possible actions is limited compared to lesser beings. A being that *cannot* do X lacks total power.

---

## 5. Failure of Sovereign Control (The Problem of Regret)

### Genesis 6:6

> *"The LORD regretted that he had made human beings on the earth, and his heart was deeply troubled."*

### 1 Samuel 15:11

> *"I regret that I have made Saul king, because he has turned away from me and has not carried out my instructions."*

* **The Analysis:** Regret requires two things: a lack of total control over how an event unfolds, and an outcome that directly opposes the entity's desire. If God were omnipotent (and omniscient), he could ensure history unfolds precisely according to his design, leaving zero structural room for regret.
* **The Subtext:** The existence of divine regret proves that human free will acts as an unmanageable barrier to divine intent. God wants one outcome, humans choose another, and God is left with a sub-optimal reality that causes him emotional distress. A sovereign who gets sabotaged by his own creation isn't operating with absolute power.
