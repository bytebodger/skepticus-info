## The Claim

The argument goes like this. The first word of the Hebrew Bible is *bereshit* (בְּרֵאשִׁית), meaning "in the beginning." It has six letters: *beyt*, *resh*, *aleph*, *shin*, *yud*, *tav*. Each Hebrew letter, the argument claims, carries a hidden pictographic meaning inherited from ancient times. When you decode those meanings, the word spells out a prophecy: "The Son of God is destroyed by his own hand on the cross." Jesus was there all along. Right in the first word. Hidden in plain sight.

It's a compelling pitch. Let's pull it apart.

---

## The Foundation: Paleo-Hebrew as a Pictographic Language

The entire argument rests on one foundational claim: that ancient Hebrew letters functioned as *pictograms* - that each letter secretly encodes the meaning of the object it once depicted.

The specific letter-to-meaning assignments being used are roughly these:

- *Beyt* = house/tent
- *Resh* = head/first person
- *Aleph* = God/ox (chief)
- *Shin* = teeth/consume/destroy
- *Yud* = hand/arm/works
- *Tav* = cross/mark

*Beyt* + *resh* together are said to form *bar*, the Aramaic word for "son." So you get: son-God-destroy-hand-cross. Which is then freely rendered as "The Son of God is destroyed by his own hand on the cross."

Now, is any of this linguistically defensible?

**No. Not according to scholars.**

The leading Hebrew scholar Michael Brown calls this approach "nonsense" and "dangerous false teaching." He explains that the ancient Hebrew script - called Paleo-Hebrew - is simply an alphabet. It has no pictographic meaning. Any linguist familiar with the material knows that by the time a writing system evolves into a true alphabet, it's no longer pictographic.

That's the critical point. Yes, the letters of the Phoenician-derived Proto-Sinaitic script evolved from earlier pictograms. An ox head became *aleph*. A picture of a house became *beyt*. But that's *etymology*, not active meaning. The same is true of the English letter "A," which descends from that same ox head. Nobody claims that every English word containing the letter A secretly encodes something about livestock.

Ron Hendel, professor of Hebrew Bible and Jewish Studies at UC-Berkeley, is blunt about the letter *shin*: "It's just a letter of the alphabet. It doesn't stand for anything except the sound 'sh.'"

No scholarly lexicon defines Hebrew words according to the alleged pictographic meanings of individual letters. This methodology doesn't appear anywhere in ancient Jewish writings - not in the Dead Sea Scrolls, not in the Targums, not in Josephus or Philo. It doesn't appear in rabbinic literature - not in the Mishnah, the Talmud, or the Midrash. This idea essentially appears out of nowhere in the last thirty years or so.

Think about what that means. Jewish scholars have been analyzing and debating every syllable of the Hebrew Bible for over two thousand years. If *bereshit* secretly encoded the crucifixion of a future messiah, someone would have noticed. Not a single ancient Jewish source makes this argument.

John Goldingay, professor of Old Testament at Fuller Theological Seminary - a Christian institution - raised exactly that point: "Why would no one have seen it for thousands of years?"

---

## The Cherry-Picking Problem

Even if we set aside the foundational linguistic problem, the argument doesn't actually work on its own terms.

When you actually look up the letter meanings in the sources these apologists rely on - including the work of Jeff Benner, whom they frequently cite - the letter *aleph* means "ox head," not "God." *Shin* means "tooth," not "destroy" or "consume." *Tav* means "mark" or even "perverse thing" - not "cross." The word "cross" doesn't appear as a meaning anywhere in the legitimate references.

So what's happening here? The advocates are picking and choosing from a wide range of *possible* meanings for each letter, selecting whichever option makes the sentence work, and then presenting the result as if it's a natural, obvious reading.

This is sometimes called the "Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy." You fire bullets at a barn wall, then walk up and draw a target around the tightest cluster. The pattern looks intentional because you created it after the fact.

The same Paleo-Hebrew script was used by Moabites, Phoenicians, and Aramaic speakers - not just Hebrews. If this pictographic decoding method were valid, it would produce equally compelling hidden messages in *all* of those other texts. Nobody attempts to apply it there. It's only invoked when the speaker is already convinced of the conclusion.

---

## The "Bar" Problem

The argument says the first two letters of *bereshit* - *beyt* and *resh* - form the word *bar*, meaning "son."

There's a problem. *Bar* is Aramaic, not Hebrew. The Hebrew word for "son" is *ben*. Genesis was written in Hebrew, not Aramaic. Using *bar* here requires sneaking in a different language to make the equation work. In actual Biblical Hebrew, *beyt-resh* doesn't mean "son."

---

## The "Jesus" Problem

Here's the deeper issue for the claim specifically about Jesus.

The Hebrew name for Jesus is *Yeshua* (יֵשׁוּעַ), or in its longer form, *Yehoshua*. Jesus is the Greek transliteration. The word *bereshit* contains no letters that spell *Yeshua* by any conventional reading. The claim isn't really that *bereshit* encodes the *name* Jesus - it's that it encodes a *sentence* about the *crucifixion*. Those are two different claims, and the "encodes Jesus" framing is loose at best.

And even on the sentence reading, you have to:

1. Accept a linguistically unsupported pictographic method
2. Cherry-pick meanings from multiple conflicting sources
3. Import an Aramaic word into a Hebrew text
4. Fill in bridging words ("is," "by his own," "on the") that aren't there
5. Ignore that the same method applied consistently would produce absurd results in any Hebrew word

---

## The Broader Pattern

This kind of argument belongs to a long tradition of *eisegesis* - reading meaning *into* a text that you've already decided is there. It's the same impulse behind Bible codes, gematria prophecies, and numerological claims. The method is infinitely flexible. You can make it say almost anything if you're willing to keep adjusting the rules mid-game.

The creator of the video most widely used to spread this claim is not a professional scholar. He's a Christian speaker. The main source he pointed to for the linguistic support was Jeff Benner - who is also not a professional scholar, but an engineer who studies ancient Hebrew as a personal interest. And even Benner himself stated clearly that he does not believe secret messages are encoded in specific words of the Bible.

The claim's primary proponent's own source doesn't support the claim.

---

## Summary

The *bereshit* argument fails at nearly every level.

The foundational linguistic premise is rejected by specialists. The letter meanings are cherry-picked and inconsistently applied. The Aramaic word for "son" is imported into a Hebrew text with no justification. The reading requires adding several words that aren't in the text. No ancient Jewish source ever made this argument. And the methodology, if applied consistently, could produce "hidden messages" in virtually any Hebrew word you choose.

What this really is - stripped of the mysticism - is a modern apologetic trick. It's dressed up in the appearance of ancient linguistic depth. But the depth isn't there when you actually dig.
