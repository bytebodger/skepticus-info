The short answer is that the texts support **both** ideas, but they do so across a historical and theological timeline. The contradiction you are picking up on is real.

If you look strictly at the text, the trajectory moves from **Jewish particularism** (Yahweh as the tribal God of Israel; Jesus as the Jewish Messiah sent to reform Israel) to **global universalism** (salvation extended to all humanity).

The biblical narrative handles this transition through a specific strategy: **particular mechanism, universal goal.** Yahweh chose a specific people not to exclude the rest of the world, but to use them as the vehicle to save it.

---

## 1. The Case for Particularism (The "Jews Only" Argument)

There is massive textual support for the idea that Yahweh is explicitly the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and that Jesus viewed his immediate mission as strictly internal to Israel.

### The Old Testament (Hebrew Bible) Focus

For most of the Hebrew Bible, Yahweh is structurally a national deity. He enters a legal contract (covenant) with one specific ethnic group.

* **The Tribal Borders:** In Deuteronomy 32:8-9, the text notes that when the Most High divided the nations, Yahweh's allocated portion was specifically Jacob (Israel).
* **The Identity:** Yahweh routinely introduces himself by lineage ("the God of Abraham...") rather than "the God of humanity." Salvation in this context meant national deliverance from Israel's enemies (Egypt, Babylon), not a ticket to heaven for global citizens.

### Jesus’ Earthly Ministry Focus

Jesus operates almost entirely within Jewish borders, enforcing Jewish law, and targeting Jewish audiences.

* **The Explicit Restriction:** In Matthew 15:24, when a non-Jewish Canaanite woman begs him for help, Jesus flatly states: *"I was sent only to the lost sheep of Israel."* When she persists, he uses a harsh cultural metaphor, comparing giving his ministry to Gentiles to throwing the children’s bread to "dogs."
* **The Direct Instructions:** When sending out his twelve disciples in Matthew 10:5-6, he commands them: *"Do not go among the Gentiles or enter any town of the Samaritans. Go rather to the lost sheep of Israel."*
* **The Number 12:** Choosing exactly twelve disciples was an explicit political and spiritual symbol aimed at reconstituting the twelve tribes of Israel, not launching a global enterprise.

---

## 2. The Case for Universalism (The "Whole World" Argument)

Conversely, there is an equally aggressive thread throughout the text arguing that Yahweh's ultimate sovereignty and salvation must encompass every human being.

### The Original Blueprint

The narrowing of God's focus to the Jews doesn't happen until Genesis 12. The first eleven chapters of the Bible (Adam, Eve, Noah) are entirely universal. They deal with humanity as a whole.

* **The Abrahamic Catch:** When Yahweh chooses Abraham in Genesis 12:3, the fine print of the contract says: *"All peoples on earth will be blessed through you."* The selection of one man was the setup for a global payout.
* **The Prophets' Vision:** Later Old Testament prophets frequently looked past Israel's borders. Isaiah 49:6 says of the coming Messiah: *"I will also make you a light for the Gentiles, that my salvation may reach to the ends of the earth."*

### The New Testament Pivot

The early Church, led by Paul, argued that Jesus’ death and resurrection effectively dissolved ethnic barriers.

* **The Final Instructions:** After the resurrection, Jesus' geographic scope changes completely. The "Great Commission" (Matthew 28:19) commands the disciples to *"go and make disciples of all nations."*
* **The Pauline Theology:** Paul spent his career fighting Jewish particularists who insisted Gentiles had to become Jewish (via circumcision and dietary laws) to be saved. In Romans 10:12, Paul writes: *"For there is no difference between Jew and Gentile—the same Lord is Lord of all."*
* **The Cosmic Scope:** Passages like John 3:16 (*"For God so loved the world..."*) and 1 John 2:2 (*"He is the atoning sacrifice for our sins, and not only for ours but also for the sins of the whole world"*) explicitly reject an ethnically gated salvation.

---

## How Scholars Reconcile the Conflict

The structural framework used by historians and theologians to make sense of these competing narratives looks like this:

| Concept | The Particularist Phase | The Universalist Phase |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Scope** | Focus on Israel ("The Lost Sheep"). | Focus on the World ("All Nations"). |
| **Mechanism** | Keep the law, protect the bloodline, reform the nation. | Faith in Christ, independent of ethnic markers. |
| **Theological Logic** | The pilot program. God establishes the covenant with a baseline community. | The global rollout. The pilot expands to the entire species. |

The consensus across mainstream biblical scholarship is that **Jesus was targeted in his deployment, but universal in his ultimate objective.** He focused exclusively on Israel during his earthly life because, according to the narrative logic of the Bible, the vehicle (Israel) had to be reformed and the messianic prophecies fulfilled before the engine could drive out to the rest of the world.

So, does the text support the idea that Yahweh is just the God of the Jews? Yes—if you stop reading at Matthew chapter 15. Does it support global salvation? Yes—if you read through the lens of Paul and the post-resurrection accounts. The narrative explicitly uses Jewish particularity to achieve global universality.
