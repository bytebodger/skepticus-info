Attributing the mass deaths caused by Pol Pot’s Khmer Rouge regime (1975–1979) to atheism is a fundamental error in historical causality. While the Khmer Rouge enforced a ruthlessly militant form of state atheism and sought to systematically obliterate all religious practices, atheism wasn't the engine driving the slaughter.

The Khmer Rouge wiped out an estimated **1.7 to 2 million people**—roughly 21% to 25% of Cambodia's population. The historical reality is that these people didn't die because Pol Pot lacked a belief in a deity; they died because of a fanatical combination of **radical agrarian communism, extreme xenophobic nationalism, and a forced regression to "Year Zero."**

---

## 1. Where State Atheism Does Bear Direct Blame

The Khmer Rouge's war on religion was absolute. Article 20 of the 1976 Constitution of Democratic Kampuchea explicitly banned all reactionary religions. In this specific dimension of the genocide, militant atheism—operationalized as a tool to destroy any loyalty outside the state—bears direct responsibility for targeted executions:

* **The Destruction of Buddhism:** Prior to 1975, Cambodia had roughly 60,000 Buddhist monks. The regime defrocked, forced into hard labor, or executed nearly all of them; only a few thousand survived. Over 3,000 Buddhist temples (*wats*) were desecrated, destroyed, or converted into granaries, prisons, and execution centers.
* **The Cham Muslim Genocide:** The Cham, an ethnic and religious minority, were brutally targeted. The regime banned the Islamic ledger, forced Chams to eat pork, executed religious leaders (imams), and murdered an estimated 36% to 40% of the Cham population.
* **The Elimination of Christians:** Though a tiny minority, Cambodian Christians were systematically hunted and executed, and the Phnom Penh cathedral was literally torn down brick by brick.

To the Khmer Rouge, religion was a "reactionary" mechanism of the old feudal order that distracted the masses from the collective will of the *Angkar* (the shadowy ruling organization).

---

## 2. Where the Blame Fails: The True Drivers of "Auto-Genocide"

While the targeted execution of religious believers accounts for tens of thousands of deaths, the overwhelming majority of the 2 million victims died from starvation, disease, exhaustion, and political purges. These were caused by structural policies completely detached from theological debates.

### Radical Agrarian Collectivization

Immediately upon seizing power in April 1975, the Khmer Rouge forced the entire urban population of Phnom Penh and other cities into the countryside. This was an attempt to instantly transition Cambodia into a classless, agrarian communist utopia.
The regime abolished currency, private property, markets, and modern medicine. Millions of urbanites, labeled "New People," were forced into slave labor on agricultural communes. They starved because the regime’s central planning was catastrophically incompetent, exporting rice to buy weapons while the domestic population withered from malnutrition.

### Hyper-Nationalism and Xenophobia

Pol Pot’s ideology wasn't just Marxist-Leninist; it was deeply infected with a fascist-style racial chauvinism. The Khmer Rouge sought a "pure" Khmer race. This led to the systematic slaughter of ethnic minorities, including ethnic Vietnamese, Chinese, and Thai residents living in Cambodia. This wasn't an atheist purge; it was ethnocentric tribalism.

### Anti-Intellectual Paranoia

The regime sought to erase history and start civilization over at "Year Zero." Anyone perceived as tainted by modern, foreign, or capitalist culture was executed. This included doctors, teachers, engineers, and anyone who spoke a foreign language or even wore eyeglasses (which was taken as a sign of literacy).

## 4. The Conceptual Failure of Blaming Atheism

To argue that atheism caused the Cambodian genocide requires treating a *negation* (the absence of belief in a god) as an *assertion* of political policy. Atheism provides no moral code, no economic model, and no geopolitical strategy. You cannot logically derive the evacuation of Phnom Penh or the execution of people who wear glasses from the proposition that God does not exist.

The Khmer Rouge didn't kill millions to validate a materialist ontology. They killed millions because they filled the religious vacuum with a secular, totalitarian cult. The *Angkar* operated exactly like an infallible, omnipotent deity:

> "The Angkar has the eyes of a pineapple and sees everything."
> "To keep you is no benefit, to destroy you is no loss."

When an ideology treats human beings as mere fuel for an abstract state machinery, mass death is the inevitable byproduct. State atheism allowed the Khmer Rouge to dismantle the existing moral frameworks that might have resisted them, but it was their radical agrarian madness and xenophobic totalitarianism that pulled the trigger.
