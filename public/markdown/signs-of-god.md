Looking at the biblical text, instances where humans explicitly initiate a request for a sign—and get it—or manage to successfully bargain with God are rare. Most signs in the Bible are initiated by God to prove a point to an unprompted or reluctant human (e.g., Moses at the burning bush).

When humans take the initiative to request a sign or negotiate terms, the text reveals a highly specific list of interactions.

---

## 1. Explicit Requests for a Sign (Granted)

To fit this category, the human must specifically ask for a visual or physical confirmation of God's word, and God must provide exactly that.

### Abraham's Torch and Smoking Firepot (Genesis 15)

* **The Request:** God promises Abraham (then Abram) that his descendants will inherit the land. Abraham asks directly: *"Sovereign Lord, how can I know that I will gain possession of it?"* (Genesis 15:8).
* **The Sign:** God instructs Abraham to cut a heifer, a goat, a ram, a dove, and a pigeon in half. That night, a smoking firepot and a blazing torch appear and pass between the pieces of the carcasses, sealing the Abrahamic Covenant.

### Gideon's Fleece (Judges 6)

This is the most famous, explicit, and multi-layered sign request in scripture. Gideon is insecure about leading Israel against the Midianites.

* **The First Request:** Gideon places a wool fleece on the threshing floor. He asks God to make the fleece wet with dew but leave the surrounding ground completely dry (Judges 6:36-38). God does it; Gideon wrings out a bowl full of water.
* **The Second Request:** Worried it was a fluke, Gideon asks again, reversing the parameters: *"Allow me just one more test with the fleece, but this time make the fleece dry and let the ground be covered with dew"* (Judges 6:39). God complies overnight.

### King Hezekiah's Sundial (2 Kings 20 / Isaiah 38)

* **The Request:** The prophet Isaiah tells King Hezekiah that God has granted him 15 more years of life after a terminal illness. Hezekiah asks for proof: *"What will be the sign that the Lord will heal me...?"* (2 Kings 20:8).
* **The Sign:** Isaiah gives Hezekiah a choice: should the shadow on the sundial move forward ten steps or backward ten steps? Hezekiah notes that moving forward is too easy, so he asks for it to go backward. The shadow moves backward ten steps on the stairway of Ahaz.

### Zechariah's Silence (Luke 1)

* **The Request:** The angel Gabriel tells the elderly priest Zechariah that his wife Elizabeth will bear a son (John the Baptist). Zechariah doubts this: *"How can I be sure of this? I am an old man and my wife is well along in years"* (Luke 1:18).
* **The Sign:** Gabriel provides a sign that doubles as a punishment for disbelief: *"And now you will be silent and not able to speak until the day this happens, because you did not believe my words"* (Luke 1:20). Zechariah remains mute until the child is born and named.

## 2. Successful Bargains / Negotiations with God

True bargaining implies a negotiation where the human proposes terms, alterable conditions, or a conditional vow, and God accepts the terms.

### Abraham Negotiates for Sodom (Genesis 18)

This is the premier text of human-divine bargaining. God reveals plans to destroy Sodom due to its systemic wickedness. Abraham steps in to negotiate based on the principle of divine justice.

* **The Negotiation:** Abraham systematically bargains the threshold for destruction down in six distinct stages (Genesis 18:23-32):
1. If there are **50** righteous? God agrees to spare it.
2. What if there are **45**? God agrees.
3. What if there are **40**? God agrees.
4. What if there are **30**? God agrees.
5. What if there are **20**? God agrees.
6. What if there are **10**? God agrees.


* **The Outcome:** The bargain is completely successful on Abraham's terms, though the city is ultimately destroyed because even ten righteous people cannot be found (only Lot and his immediate family).

### Jephthah's Tragic Vow (Judges 11)

* **The Bargain:** Jephthah is leading Israel against the Ammonites. He proposes a strict quid-pro-quo deal to God: *"If you give the Ammonites into my hands, whatever comes out of the door of my house to meet me when I return in triumph... will be the Lord’s, and I will sacrifice it as a burnt offering"* (Judges 11:30-31).
* **The Outcome:** God fulfills His side of the deal, granting victory. Jephthah's bargain succeeds, but it turns tragic when his daughter is the first to walk out of his house to greet him.

### Hannah's Vow for a Son (1 Samuel 1)

* **The Bargain:** Hannah is barren and deeply distressed. She prays at the tabernacle and proposes a conditional contract: *"Lord Almighty, if you will only look on your servant’s misery and remember me... and give her a son, then I will give him to the Lord for all the days of his life..."* (1 Samuel 1:11).
* **The Outcome:** God accepts the terms. Hannah conceives and gives birth to Samuel, whom she delivers to Eli the priest to serve in the tabernacle once he is weaned.

## Edge Cases & Clarifications

To keep this analysis textually accurate, a few famous accounts must be distinguished because they do not technically fit your exact criteria:

* **Moses and the Signs (Exodus 4):** Moses gets the signs of his staff turning into a snake and his hand becoming leprous. However, Moses *did not ask for these*. Moses was arguing that he was incompetent (*"What if they do not believe me?"*). God initiated the signs to equip a reluctant messenger.
* **Gideon's Fire (Judges 6:21):** Before the fleece incident, Gideon asks for a sign that he is truly speaking to God. He brings an offering of meat and bread. The Angel of God touches it with a staff, and fire flares from the rock to consume it. This fits the "asked and received" criteria perfectly as a precursor to the fleece.
* **The Fleece of Abraham's Servant (Genesis 24):** Eliezer (Abraham's servant) asks for a specific situational sign to find a wife for Isaac: the right woman will offer to water his camels too. Rebekah does exactly that. This is a requested and granted sign, though requested by an unnamed proxy agent rather than a primary biblical protagonist.
