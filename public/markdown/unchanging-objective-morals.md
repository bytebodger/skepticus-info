This is a genuinely rich question, and it cuts to the heart of one of the most important tensions in Christian moral theology. Let's work through it carefully and systematically.

---

## When God's Laws Change: A Textual Analysis

### Framing the Problem Precisely

The claim being examined isn't just that human *interpretation* of God's laws has shifted over time. That's obvious and uncontroversial. The stronger claim - and the one that creates real problems for divine command theories of objective morality - is that **the laws themselves, as presented in the text, change**. God commands something. Then God commands something different. Or Jesus explicitly revises what Moses said. Or Paul declares obsolete what the Torah declared eternal.

If morality is objective because it flows from the unchanging nature of God, these changes require explanation. Either:

1. God's nature changed - which most theologians reject outright.
2. The earlier commands were never really moral absolutes - which raises questions about why they were framed as such.
3. The commands were always provisional and contextual - which concedes significant ground to moral relativism.
4. Later commands *fulfill* rather than contradict earlier ones - the standard Christian response, which we'll examine honestly.

Let's go through the major categories of change.

---

### Category 1: The Dietary Laws

Few areas of biblical law are as explicit, detailed, and subsequently abandoned as the dietary code.

Leviticus 11 and Deuteronomy 14 give exhaustive lists of clean and unclean animals. The prohibition on pork, shellfish, and numerous other foods is stated flatly and without qualification. Leviticus 11:7-8 on pigs: *"You shall not eat their flesh, and you shall not touch their carcasses; they are unclean to you."* This is framed as a holiness requirement - these laws exist because God is holy and Israel must be holy to belong to him.

Then Acts 10 happens.

Peter receives a vision in which a sheet descends containing all kinds of animals - including unclean ones - and a voice says: *"Get up, Peter. Kill and eat."* Peter objects: *"Surely not, Lord! I have never eaten anything impure or unclean."* The voice responds: *"Do not call anything impure that God has made clean."*

This happens three times. The text then applies this vision to the inclusion of Gentiles - but the immediate and plain content of the vision is that the food laws have been revoked.

Paul is even more direct. Romans 14:14: *"I am convinced, being fully persuaded in the Lord Jesus, that nothing is unclean in itself."* Colossians 2:16: *"Do not let anyone judge you by what you eat or drink."* 1 Timothy 4:4: *"Everything God created is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with thanksgiving."*

Now - why does this matter for the morality question?

The dietary laws weren't presented as temporary health regulations or cultural customs. They were framed as holiness requirements rooted in God's own nature. Leviticus 11:44-45 explicitly grounds them theologically: *"I am the Lord your God; consecrate yourselves and be holy, because I am holy."* The law isn't "don't eat pork because it's unhealthy." It's "don't eat pork because I am holy."

If God's holiness is the justification for the law, and God's holiness doesn't change, then on what basis does the law change? The standard response - that these laws were "shadows" pointing to Christ and are now fulfilled - is a retrospective theological interpretation. The text of Leviticus gives no hint that these are temporary. It presents them as binding obligations rooted in eternal divine character.

---

### Category 2: The Sabbath

The Sabbath command is not a minor procedural rule. It's one of the Ten Commandments - the foundational moral code of the Hebrew tradition. Exodus 20:8-11 grounds it in creation itself: God rested on the seventh day, therefore Israel must rest on the seventh day. Numbers 15:32-36 describes a man executed for gathering sticks on the Sabbath. This is capital punishment for what amounts to picking up firewood.

The New Testament's relationship with the Sabbath is complicated and ultimately transformative.

Jesus repeatedly violates Sabbath restrictions - healing on the Sabbath, allowing his disciples to pluck grain on the Sabbath - and when challenged, offers a principle that reframes the entire command: *"The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath"* (Mark 2:27). That's a significant hermeneutical move. It shifts the Sabbath from a divine requirement (something God needs from humans) to a human benefit (something God gave humans for their sake). Those are philosophically very different things.

Paul in Colossians 2:16-17 lists the Sabbath among the things that are now "a shadow of the things that were to come; the reality, however, is found in Christ." Hebrews 4 reinterprets the Sabbath entirely as a metaphor for spiritual rest in Christ.

Most Christians today worship on Sunday, not Saturday. Sunday is not the Sabbath. The explicit command in the Ten Commandments is to observe the *seventh* day - Saturday. The shift to Sunday was gradual and institutionalized by the early church, justified theologically as the "Lord's Day" (the day of resurrection), but with no direct biblical command mandating the switch.

Here's the problem this creates: if the Ten Commandments represent the core of God's objective moral law - as many Christians claim - and one of those Ten Commandments has been effectively reinterpreted to the point of being unrecognizable in practice, then "the Ten Commandments are God's objective moral law" is a much more complicated claim than it sounds.

---

### Category 3: The Sermon on the Mount's Direct Revisions

Matthew 5 is the most explicit example of legal revision in the entire New Testament. Jesus doesn't just reinterpret Moses. He directly revises him - six times - using the formula: *"You have heard that it was said... but I tell you."*

Let's go through each one.

**On murder:** The law said don't murder. Jesus says anger itself is the moral equivalent of murder. Calling someone a fool makes you liable to hellfire. This is a significant expansion - the legal standard becomes internal and psychological, not just behavioral.

**On adultery:** The law said don't commit adultery. Jesus says looking at a woman with lust is already adultery in the heart. Again, an internal psychological state becomes equivalent to an external act.

**On divorce:** This one is the most legally direct revision. *"It was said, 'Anyone who divorces his wife must give her a certificate of divorce.' But I tell you that anyone who divorces his wife, except for sexual immorality, makes her the victim of adultery."* Deuteronomy 24:1-4 explicitly permitted divorce. Jesus is here overturning a Mosaic permission - not just adding nuance to it, but reversing it.

**On oaths:** The law said don't break your oaths. Jesus says don't swear oaths at all. *"Let your 'Yes' be 'Yes,' and your 'No,' 'No'."* The law regulated oath-swearing. Jesus abolishes it.

**On retaliation:** *"You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.' But I tell you, do not resist an evil person."* The *lex talionis* - the law of proportional retaliation - appears in Exodus 21:24, Leviticus 24:20, and Deuteronomy 19:21. It appears three times in the Torah, suggesting it wasn't a minor legal footnote. Jesus doesn't refine it. He inverts it entirely.

**On enemies:** *"You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.' But I tell you, love your enemies."* The scope of the moral obligation is dramatically expanded.

Now, the standard Christian response to all of this is that Jesus is *fulfilling* the law, not abolishing it - he even says so in Matthew 5:17. And in a meaningful sense that's true: he's getting at the spirit behind the law rather than just the letter.

But here's the logical tension that response creates. If Jesus is revealing what the law *really meant all along*, then Moses - and God through Moses - communicated the law inadequately. The people who followed the eye-for-an-eye principle were following what God explicitly told them to follow. If what God *really* wanted was non-retaliation, then God's communication of that intention was a failure for roughly 1,500 years of Israelite history.

Alternatively, if the law genuinely changed - if the ethical standard for non-retaliation is genuinely different from the ethical standard for proportional justice - then moral standards changed. Either God communicated poorly for 1,500 years, or the moral standard shifted. Those are both uncomfortable options.

---

### Category 4: Circumcision

Circumcision is not a minor ceremonial detail. In Genesis 17, God establishes it as the sign of the covenant with Abraham. The language is absolute. Genesis 17:14: *"Any uncircumcised male, who has not been circumcised in the flesh, will be cut off from his people; he has broken my covenant."*

This is covenant-breaking language. Failure to circumcise doesn't just violate a rule. It breaks the foundational relationship between the individual and God.

Then Paul writes Galatians.

Galatians 5:2: *"Mark my words! I, Paul, tell you that if you let yourselves be circumcised, Christ will be of no value to you at all."* Galatians 5:6: *"In Christ Jesus neither circumcision nor uncircumcision has any value."* Philippians 3:2: Paul calls the circumcision party "dogs" and "mutilators of the flesh."

The Jerusalem Council in Acts 15 formally decides that Gentile Christians are not required to be circumcised. James, Peter, and Paul all agree.

So: something that God described as a covenant requirement - the absence of which constituted *breaking the covenant* - has been declared not just optional but potentially counterproductive. The thing that once defined membership in God's people now has "no value."

The standard theological response is that physical circumcision was always a sign pointing to something spiritual - "circumcision of the heart" (Deuteronomy 30:6, Romans 2:29). The physical sign has been superseded by its spiritual reality.

But this retrospective reinterpretation is doing enormous work. Nothing in Genesis 17 suggests circumcision is provisional or symbolic in a way that makes it dispensable. It's presented as a permanent, binding covenant sign. The word "everlasting" appears explicitly: *"my covenant in your flesh is to be an everlasting covenant"* (Genesis 17:13).

If "everlasting covenant" means "until Christ comes and renders it obsolete," the word "everlasting" is being used in an unusual way that requires external theological justification - justification the text itself doesn't supply.

---

### Category 5: Animal Sacrifice

The sacrificial system occupies enormous real estate in the Torah. Leviticus is largely a detailed manual for animal sacrifice - the types of offerings, the procedures, the occasions. This wasn't peripheral religious activity. It was the central mechanism of atonement and relationship with God.

The entire book of Hebrews exists to argue that Jesus's death supersedes the sacrificial system. Hebrews 10:1-4: *"The law is only a shadow of the good things that are coming - not the realities themselves. For this reason it can never, by the same sacrifices repeated endlessly year after year, make perfect those who draw near to worship."* Hebrews 10:11-12 contrasts the repeated animal sacrifices with Christ's single sufficient sacrifice.

Animal sacrifice is now considered not just unnecessary but theologically inappropriate for Christians. The entire edifice of Levitical sacrifice - which God commanded in extraordinary detail - has been dismantled.

The "shadow and fulfillment" framework handles this more comfortably than some other examples - the sacrificial system pointing forward to Christ is a coherent theological narrative. But notice what it concedes: God instituted a system of atonement through animal sacrifice that was always, by design, inadequate. Hebrews says explicitly that animal blood *could never* take away sins. So for the entire period of Israelite history, people were practicing a system of atonement that wasn't actually achieving atonement in the ultimate sense.

That raises a question that's rarely asked directly: if God always intended the sacrificial system as a temporary placeholder, why give the impression - through the extraordinary detail and the holiness requirements surrounding it - that this was the actual mechanism of forgiveness? The gap between what the Torah presents and what Hebrews says it "really was" is wide.

---

### Category 6: Laws That Are Simply Ignored

Some Old Testament laws don't get explicitly revised by the New Testament. They just get quietly abandoned - often without explanation. These are worth cataloguing because their abandonment is itself revealing.

**Stoning for various offenses:** Deuteronomy 22:23-24 mandates stoning for a woman who doesn't cry out during rape within city limits. Deuteronomy 21:18-21 mandates stoning for a persistently rebellious son. Leviticus 20:10 mandates death for adultery. Leviticus 20:13 mandates death for male same-sex relations. These are capital offenses in the Torah. No Christian denomination practices them. No New Testament passage explicitly revokes them for many of these cases. They've simply been set aside.

**Slavery regulations:** Exodus 21, Leviticus 25, and Deuteronomy 15 all contain detailed regulations governing slavery - including the permanent ownership of slaves, the conditions under which slaves can be beaten, and the distinction between Israelite debt slavery and permanent foreign slavery. Leviticus 25:44-46 explicitly permits buying foreigners as permanent property passed down to children. These are not condemned in the New Testament - Paul in Ephesians 6:5 and Colossians 3:22 actually instructs slaves to obey their masters. The abolition of slavery was driven by moral arguments that eventually overrode these texts, but those arguments came largely from *outside* the biblical framework, or from a general principle of human dignity that required overriding specific biblical commands.

**Genocide commands:** Deuteronomy 7:1-2 and 20:16-17 command the complete destruction of the Canaanite nations - men, women, children. 1 Samuel 15:3 has Samuel telling Saul to kill *"men and women, children and infants, cattle and sheep, camels and donkeys."* These commands are not revised in the New Testament. They're simply not addressed. Most Christians handle them through interpretive strategies - they were unique commands for a unique historical context, they're not moral precedents - but that handling is itself a form of moral reasoning that operates *above* the text rather than *from* it.

---

### Category 7: Love as the Superseding Principle

Paul's formulation in Romans 13:8-10 and Galatians 5:14 makes an extraordinary claim: *"Love your neighbor as yourself... Love is the fulfillment of the law."*

This is simultaneously the most elegant resolution of the problem and the most revealing.

If love is the fulfillment of the law - if the entire Mosaic code can be reduced to a single principle of neighbor-love - then the specific commands of the Torah were always instrumental, not intrinsic. They weren't themselves the moral substance. They were attempts to embody a deeper principle in a particular cultural and historical context.

That's actually a sophisticated and defensible moral position. It resembles rule utilitarianism - specific rules exist to serve a deeper value, and the rules can be revised when they no longer serve that value in changed circumstances.

But here's what it concedes: the specific biblical laws are not themselves the objective moral standard. They're approximations of it. The objective moral standard - if there is one - is something like "love" or "human flourishing" or "the good," and the specific commands are context-dependent applications of that standard.

That concession is significant. Because it means Christians who say "the Bible gives us objective morality" are actually pointing to an underlying principle that the Bible imperfectly and provisionally expresses - not to the specific commands as such.

And if the underlying principle is "love," then the moral authority of any specific biblical command depends on whether it genuinely serves love in a given context. Which is exactly the kind of moral reasoning that doesn't require a divine text to perform.

### The Standard Responses and Their Limits

It's worth taking the major Christian responses seriously before drawing conclusions.

**"The Old Testament law was for Israel, not for all humans."**
This is the *covenant* distinction - Mosaic law was the national constitution of Israel, not a universal moral code. It's a reasonable interpretive framework. But it creates a problem: if the specific Mosaic laws weren't universal moral obligations, then God's command to stone adulterers wasn't a universal moral truth - it was a culturally specific regulation. That's a concession to moral contextualism, not a defense of objective morality.

**"Jesus fulfills the law rather than abolishing it."**
The fulfillment language in Matthew 5:17 is real and important. But as argued above, "fulfillment" that renders specific commands inoperative - or inverts them - is doing more work than the word normally carries. Fulfillment that makes something obsolete is, from a practical standpoint, indistinguishable from revision.

**"The moral law remains; only the ceremonial and civil law changes."**
This is the classic Reformed distinction between moral law (eternal), ceremonial law (pointing to Christ), and civil law (for Israel's nationhood). It's the most systematic attempt to handle the problem.

But the distinction is imposed on the text, not derived from it. The Torah doesn't label its own laws as "moral," "ceremonial," or "civil." The Ten Commandments include Sabbath observance - is that moral or ceremonial? Different Christian traditions answer differently. The prohibition on mixing fabrics (Leviticus 19:19) - ceremonial? Almost certainly. But what's the principled basis for that categorization that doesn't involve post-hoc reasoning?

More importantly: the Sermon on the Mount revises laws that would clearly qualify as "moral" under any reasonable definition - the retaliation principle, the treatment of enemies. If the moral law doesn't change and Jesus is revising moral laws, the framework breaks down.

---

### The Honest Conclusion

The biblical evidence, taken seriously on its own terms, shows:

- Explicit legal reversals (dietary laws, circumcision requirements).
- Direct revisions by Jesus of Torah commands (divorce, retaliation, oath-swearing).
- The wholesale obsolescence of major systems (sacrifice, Levitical priesthood).
- The quiet abandonment of commands that were never formally revised.
- A meta-principle (love) that supersedes specific commands and implicitly relativizes them.

The theological frameworks developed to handle these changes are sophisticated and not without merit. But they all require reading the texts through lenses that the texts themselves don't supply. The Torah doesn't say "these are temporary until the Messiah comes." Genesis 17 doesn't say "circumcision is a shadow of something better." Leviticus doesn't label its own laws as ceremonial versus moral.

The changes are real. What's contested is their *significance* - whether they represent genuine revisions to God's moral commands, or a progressive revelation of a single consistent underlying standard.

For the claim that "Christian morality is objective" to be coherent, it needs to be grounded in that underlying standard - something like the character of God, or the principle of love, or human flourishing - rather than in the specific biblical commands, which demonstrably shift.

If that underlying standard is the real moral bedrock, then it's worth asking how we access and apply it - and whether a 2,000-year-old text is the necessary vehicle for doing so.

That's not a question the text itself answers.
